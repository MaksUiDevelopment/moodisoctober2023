Is Israel Employing Disproportionate Force Against HAMAS?
The next alleged war crime committed by Israel is the use of disproportionate force. It appears to be a war crime, doesn't it? Even if Israel does not intentionally target civilians, it is challenging to deny the number of casualties. We know that around 1400 people have died on the Israeli side, while the Gaza Ministry of Health reports significantly higher numbers on the Gaza side. It sounds official and lawful, but we are aware that Gaza is controlled by HAMAS, the Minister of Health in Gaza is affiliated with HAMAS—the same HAMAS that kills Israel's civilians. Therefore, we should not take their numbers at face value.

But let's momentarily assume that there are honest and kind people in Gaza's healthcare system, and their numbers are accurate. The figures they provide are high, around 10,000, while Israel's casualties are ≈1400. Does this align with the legal definition of a war crime?

When it comes to proportionality, it's not about revenge or killing an equal number of civilians. In other words, it's not "an eye for an eye," not about committing exactly the same actions or even in the number of bodies. When you judge the appropriateness of an attack based on the number of casualties, you are not protecting civilians; you are incentivizing the use of human shields.

HAMAS is indeed adept at public relations. They know they have a "compelling narrative." The more civilians die, the more they can say, "Hey, we have the right to attack Israel," and that's why they have an incentive to use women and children as human shields. The more civilian casualties, the more legitimate HAMAS appears. It's harsh, but it's the truth. It was in their plans on October 7th, evident from where they store weapons and where attacks originate (from residential buildings and densely populated areas).

There's a good analogy: if you're trying to eliminate a sniper in a mission, you can't level an entire city, but you can destroy a building, even if there are civilians inside. The same goes for HAMAS leaders; it would be proportionate to destroy a residential building to eliminate a HAMAS leader. The most challenging part here is the lack of numbers, lack of ratios, and this means that for those making such decisions, it's not easy.
Did Israel create a humanitarian catastrophe?
The most challenging part remains: does Israel prevent Palestinians in the Gaza Strip from receiving humanitarian aid? It sounds serious, but does Israel fit the legal definition of this war crime? We know that immediately after the start of the war, Israel cut off electricity, water, and internet. While these actions may be directed against Hamas, they also harm innocent civilians. The residents of Gaza already suffer from a lack of adequate infrastructure and food independence. They lack proper sewage, sufficient electricity, and easy access to clean water. For the past 17 years, Hamas has spent its resources on acquiring rockets and weapons instead of modernizing sewage systems, improving living standards, and building new water purification and power facilities. Worse still, they have stolen crucial resources needed to operate the internal power station. So, is this blockade a war crime? A blockade or siege like the one in Gaza is not prohibited by international law. Of course, the law has humanitarian aspects, right? Such as the transportation of necessary fuel, food, and medicine to peaceful civilians across the border. But this is only applicable when they can be reliably delivered without redirecting them to the enemy. About a month ago, the UN confirmed that Hamas had stolen these shipments. Therefore, the answer to the first question posed in this paragraph is no; Israel did not commit such a war crime.
